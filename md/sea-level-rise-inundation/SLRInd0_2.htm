Description:
Abstract:
The 2 Foot Sea Level Rise Inundation dataset is a derivative of high-resolution topographic data (LiDAR) that the State of Maryland in cooperation with local and federal agencies has acquired to identify areas vulnerable to inundation and flooding. The dataset represents inundation areas of Maryland coastal counties in the event of a two foot sea level rise. Data is not available for Harford Co., Prince George's Co., and Baltimore City.
Purpose:
Through a National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, Coastal Zone Management Grant, the 2 Foot Sea Level Rise Inundation dataset was created to assist the Maryland Commission on Climate Change achieve one of its mandates: developing a strategy for reducing Maryland's vulnerability to climate change-induced sea level rise. The dataset was created as a generalized projection map for the State that is based on high resolution information.

Data_Quality_Information:
Logical_Consistency_Report:
Each pixel with a value of 1 is considered water while each pixel with a value of 0 is land or areas where no LiDAR data was available at the time of the project. Inland areas, such as quarries, classified as water but not likely to be influence by sea level rise were reclassified as land. A clump process, which passed a 3x3 filter over the image to compare each pixel to its 8 neighboring pixels, was employed to group adjacent pixels of the same value (0 or 1). The resultant clumped image, which identified groups of contiguous water or land pixels, was then passed through a sieve process which eliminated all groups of contiguous pixels with an area less than 0.25 acres.
Completeness_Report:
The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University performed various inspections to verify that the dataset represents areas inundated by a two foot sea level rise. Comparisons were made between the dataset's source LiDAR data and the reclassification values used to create the final dataset.

Process_Step:
Process_Description:
Data Collection: Two primary data sources were obtained for this project: 1) high resolution LiDAR data and 2) datum information for select NOAA tidal stations. The high resolution LiDAR data provided elevation values as measured from the NAVD 88 datum. The elevation baseline for this project was Mean Sea Level. Therefore, NOAA tidal station data was required in order to determine the difference between the tidal station's NAVD 88 datum and Mean Sea Level so that the final inundation areas are based on a Mean Sea Level baseline not a NAVD 88 baseline.

The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University obtained high-resolution LiDAR data for Anne Arundel, Caroline, Cecil, Charles, Dorchester, Kent, Queen Anne's, Somerset, St. Mary's, Talbot, Wicomico, and Worcester Counties from the MD DNR. Baltimore County and Calvert County LiDAR data was obtained from Baltimore County's Office of Information Technology and Calvert County's Department of Technology Services respectively. LIDAR DATA FOR BALTIMORE CITY, HARFORD COUNTY, AND PRINCE GEORGE'S COUNTY WAS NOT AVAILABLE AT THE TIME THIS DATASET WAS CREATED.

The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University obtained point data representing select tidal stations from NOAA's Tides and Currents website (<http://tidesandcurrents.noaa.gov/station_retrieve.shtml?type=Historic+Tide+Data>). Tidal station data used for this project include Baltimore (Fort McHenry), Beaverdam Creek, Bishops Head, Cambridge, Chesapeake City, Chincoteague Channel, Colonial Beach, Crumpton, Havre De Grace, Hillsboro, Lewisetta, Lower Marlboro, McCreadys Creek, Ocean City Inlet, Ocean City Fishing Pier, Snow Hill, Solomons Island, Tangier Island, Tolchester Beach, U.S. Naval Academy, Vienna, and Washington.

Process_Date: 200708
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
LiDAR Processing: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University reprojected the Calvert County LiDAR data from Maryland State Plane (NAD 83 Feet) to Maryland State Plane (NAD 83 Meters), resampled the dataset's spatial resolution from 3 feet to 2 meters, and converted the dataset's pixel values from feet to meters in order to conform the dataset to the MD DNR and Baltimore County LiDAR datasets. Subsequently, the MD DNR, Baltimore County, and Calvert County LiDAR datasets were mosaiced into five mosaics which together encompassed most of the study area, excluding those areas where LIDAR data was not available: Baltimore City, Harford County and Prince George's County.
Process_Date: 200708
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
Thiessen Polygon Creation: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University created a tidal station thiessen polygon dataset from the tidal station point dataset. The tidal station polygon boundaries were manually altered to make the polygons cover areas likely influenced by tides in a similar manner as the tidal station the polygon is derived from.
Process_Date: 200703
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
LiDAR Clipping: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University clipped the five LiDAR mosaics created in process step two by each tidal station polygon. This resulted in 22 LiDAR datasets, one corresponding to the extents of each tidal station polygon created in process step three.
Process_Date: 200708
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
Reclassification Value Calculation: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University calculated three reclassification values (2 foot, 5 foot, 10 foot) for each tide station's LiDAR dataset. Each reclassification value was calculated by adding the difference between the tide station's NAVD 88 value and Mean Sea Level value to the flood inundation value (i.e. 2 feet, 5 feet, or 10 feet). It is important to note that 13 of the 22 tide stations did not have a NAVD 88 value. An estimated difference between NAVD 88 and Mean Sea Level at these 13 tide stations was calculated based on the known differences of the other 9 tide stations. The estimated difference assigned to the 13 tide stations was 0.02 meters, which was calculated by averaging 7 out of the 9 known differences (the highest and lowest known differences were considered outliers and not used in the average).
Process_Date: 200708
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
LiDAR Reclassification: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University reclassified each tidal station's LiDAR dataset by the reclassification values that were calculated in process step 5. This produced three 1-bit raster datasets for each tide station: a 2 foot inundation raster, a 5 foot inundation raster, and a 10 foot inundation raster. Each inundation raster dataset was clipped by the given tide station's boundary in order to remove incorrect water classifications outside of the tide station's boundary.
Process_Date: 200709
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
Minimum Mapping Unit Established: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University conformed the three reclassified raster datasets for each tide station to the 0.25 acre minimum mapping unit. Each reclassified raster dataset's pixels were clumped, based on 8 neighboring pixels, and all clumped pixels less than 0.25 acres were subsequently removed through a sieving process. The sieved reclassified raster datasets were converted to 1-bit images.
Process_Date: 200709
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
Statewide Inundation Raster Datasets Created: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University mosaiced the inundation raster datasets created in process step 7 to produce three 1-bit statewide inundation raster datasets, where one represents a 2 foot sea level rise, another represents a 5 foot sea level rise, and another represents a 10 foot sea level rise.
Process_Date: 200709
Process_Step:
Process_Description:
Classification Review: The Center for Geographic Information Sciences at Towson University reviewed each sea level rise inundation raster's classification. Several areas incorrectly classified as water were reclassified as land and a number of quarries located in areas distant from the true inundation areas were also reclassified as land.
